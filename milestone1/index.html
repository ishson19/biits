Milestone 1

Problem & Solutions

The goal of our project is to create an educational web application to teach kids with autism spectrum disorder (ASD) about colors. Games make learning fun, motivates kids, and helps them pay attention and stay focused on the subject. Educational games also encourage students to learn in their own environment. There are many websites that promote online learning games, but few of them cater specifically for children with autism. Autistic children are often hypersensitive to sensory input, which makes sensory overload more likely. Some common symptoms of sensory overload include difficulty focusing due to competing sensory input, irritability, restlessness and discomfort, stress, fear, anxiety, and urge to shield eyes from the sensory input. With competing sensory input, an individual will feel as though they are stuck and do not know what to focus on [1]. Because autistic children generally have trouble identifying colors, it is important to develop an environment where they do not feel overwhelmed by color schemes [2].

We are creating a web application that is soothing and encourages them to learn without overwhelming them. The main color scheme of the website will be simplistic and only use a few colors with low intensity in order to not discourage the kids. One game we will make is a color game that presents kids with autism a forum where they can learn to distinguish between colors. The game will encourage these kids to pick the correct color asked of them without excessive distractions such as too many bright colors on the screen or constantly moving flashy objects that could be visually straining. The game will also reward correct answers as positive reinforcement is especially effective for kids with autism. This game can be beneficial because it gives kids with autism a productive learning environment that encourages them to learn and focus without burdening them.

Problems with Existing Solutions

From our experience searching online, it is clear there are few sites catered specifically to autistic children's use. Many sites will have resources to help autistic kids in person, or have print-outs to use with the child. There are not many that prioritize online games specifically for helping autistic kids learn. There are countless game sites online, and some built for teaching young children. However, after extensive searching there was exactly one site dedicated to helping autistic kids that was free (autismgames.com.au). It had five games, none of which related to color. The games were frustratingly slow and did not have clear instructions. After exploring the site, we realized that the site just aggregated games from a different site that did not mention autism, and didn’t even have a game for each category represented. In general, there are very few free interactive ways to teach children with ASD about colors online.

While creating an educational environment for children with ASD, it is vital to keep color schemes in mind. The main pages of the site we found actually does a decent job of avoiding overstimulation. This is one of the most important considerations in design, as it is the main problem with most educational and game sites. However, the design should be aesthetically pleasing and interesting. The green, grey, and white design of the site does not foster creativity or healthy excitement. We must find a balance between peaceful, soothing designs and fun designs that will excite children. Essentially, we want to avoid being too boring or too flashy.

Users

The primary users that our application interacts with is young children diagnosed with autism. The target age group are children too young for kindergarten, with a typical age range of 2 to 5 years. Since the differences between high functioning and low functioning individuals are not as pronounced at a younger age, we aim to make the website cater to both groups. In addition to this, children diagnosed with autism are highly encouraged to learn through visual means at an early age to build a foundation of knowledge. This age group is primarily limited to being users that would use their parent or guardian’s iPad or mobile phone to interact with the website through its touch screen interface. These mobile users may be in their own homes, pre-K, or other childcare facilities when using the app. Children that are not on the spectrum may not fully benefit from this app, but it can provide them with a visual focused alternative to learning the provided topics and achieving developmental milestones common to every individual. Gaining access to this app from this user would require it to be initially accessed from a different user group, typically their parent, guardian, or childcare worker.

The second set of users are parents or guardians of the children interacting with the app. These individual’s primary interaction with the website is viewing the child’s progress in a topic area. These individuals are capable of accessing the website through desktops or laptops on top of mobile devices. Due to this, the application must be responsive to both desktops and various sizes of mobile devices. Online education websites for children with autism primarily run on subscription or payment plans, but this application is catered to families that are in low income areas. While geographic location of these families is not a notable factor in identifying users, there are certain economic status conditions that may be required. Families or childcare providers are financially expected to have access to a mobile device. This, coupled with the possible low hardware specifications in low income areas, require a low resource intensive model.

Notable groups that this web app does not cater to is older and more mature audiences, individuals that have a proper understanding of the materials being taught, and individuals that are color blind. For the first two, the primary focus of the app is to target younger individuals with autism that have little knowledge of the topics presented. In regard to color blind individuals, the colors chosen are optimized for the hyper-sensitive nature that autism presents within children.

This target group is appropriate as it focuses our app toward a specific demographic that takes into account age, technical understanding, economic standing, and point of access. While our primary demographic is children, we consider the perspective of others around them such as their parents, guardians, or other childcare workers. We also understood the limited scope of users for our project and identified that this project may not be suitable for all visitors. Children not on the spectrum may not fully benefit from the lessons or teaching methods provided, but these users can see this application more as an alternative style to teaching that is more visual than textual.

Task Analysis


The users will be young children, specifically geared towards children 2-5 years old who are diagnosed with autism. The application is simple and provides productive learning experience for this target group as studies have shown early intervention is optimal for this age group. By using simple benchmarks and milestones, young children will benefit from the application. The application can be used on a smartphone, tablet, or desktop computer and will require secure internet connection at the device’s location. The application will likely be used at home or school. Low-income families can benefit from this application as the application will be free and accessible on a variety of devices.

1. Parent or teacher will create an account when first opening an account on the application
     a. (1.1) Parent or teacher will ensure that the device (mobile, tablet, or computer) has secure, stable WiFi access at the location the device is at (home, school)
     b. (1.2) Parent or teacher will provide an email, first name of child, and password to create an account
     c. (1.3) Parent or teacher will get a user confirmation via email notification
2. Child will open the application on a tablet or computer with adult supervision
     a. (2.1) The parent or teacher will provide child with a tablet or a computer
     b. (2.2) The parent or teacher will download the app or go to the link on the preferred device
         1. (2.2.1) The parent or teacher will login into the application using the child’s credentials
          1. (2.2.1.1) If the adult has forgotten the password, they can reset it using their email
         2. (2.2.1.2) Adult can choose to remain logged in on the current device
3. Child or the adult will choose a topic or game on the application’s game portal
     a. (3.1) The parent or teacher will select the game that best fits the need of the child
     b. (3.2) The child will select a game while browsing through different topics
         1. (3.1.1) The topic will include educational games focused on elementary education
         2. (3.1.2) The education games will contain content from essential developmental benchmarks identified by unique curriculum designed for autistic children
4. Child will play the game to make progress on developmental benchmarks
     a. (4.1) Child will receive rewards based on their achievements
         1. (4.1.1) Parent or teacher can view the achievements to track child’s progress
     b. (4.2) The game will provide a productive learning environment for the child to learn about selected topic (identifying colors, primary versus secondary colors)

The application, where the child will complete their tasks, must be easy and simple to navigate. Since the application is aimed for autistic children, the application should avoid having bright colors, flashing objects, and other distracting UI.

Implications

From our research, we learned that in order to cater to the needs of our target users we need to avoid color palettes that overload sensory inputs. Our research regarding the sensitivity of sensory input within autistic children has shown that even the slightest stimulation in certain colors, visuals, auditory sounds, and other sensations may be disruptive and anxiety inducing for these children. Additionally, based on further research and numerous experiments conducted by professional doctors in the field, autistic children can be motivated to learn if provided with consistent rewards to help encourage progress. When designing our application, we have to consider avoid flashy, moving objects and have a reward system/incentives to keep the kids encouraged to learn[3]. Based on interviews with special needs teachers, we were able to learn that various users of this target group respond to all colors differently on a spectrum. Some children may love certain colors at high intensity, and others may find those colors to be blinding and distracting. It is critical that we create capabilities for the users/parents to mute or increase the intensity of certain colors presented on the website, as well as modify the color scheme to suit the child’s own preferences. This can also work as an “incentive” for the child. Our final goal is to be able to implement simple navigation given the target/age group to help with the overall experience of the application, while fostering independence.
